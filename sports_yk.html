Crafting an NBA Q&A Experience
through Alexa

Interaction Design, Prototyping, Information Design, Visual Design, User Research

Introduction

The Yahoo Search organization is invested in crafting in new interaction paradigms to graduate from the traditional search box. One of our natural language teams is interested in digging into providing nuanced answers in a conversational medium whether for voice or text. Their current focus is on elevating the treatment of NBA entities.

I wanted to help uncover design guidelines through rapid design experimentation with Alexa across modalities for the NBA use case. Here, I will provide broad brush strokes on how I designed for voice-first answers for rich pre/during/post-game questions.


Q&A for NBA Entities

Yahoo has a wealth of sports data that we want users to access in natural ways. For the most part, agents out in the market can only answer surface-level basketball queries. Our backend will negotiate answers from multiple sources of truth in order to provide finessed answers.

Here is a rough depiction of how a typical sports fan may request news throughout the day. I'm currently working with the User Experience Research team to deeply understand the specific user contexts (e.g. times of day, question needs, devices used) in order to design appropriate answer formats across devices.



Designing an Answer

Given the inherent difficulties with comprehension associated with VUI, I wanted to understand what it means to make a concise yet precise voice-only answer. I decided to make rules of required parameters for all user intent types. The answer can vary in terms of values and "decoration" (e.g. if the team wanted to add in an extra fact nugget), otherwise, the ratio of filled parameters to the length of the answer string needs to be optimal.

Progressive Enhancement

While primarily concentrated on VUI design and ensuring a correct and concise answer in that medium, I am also considering the cases when users could be using screens for various scenarios. We have experimented with different card formats depending on the intent and various lengths of information in graphical form compared to voice.

Lessons Learned

  1. I have to be sensitive to a user's familiarity with assistants; there aren't a whole lot of users that are, let alone think highly of their capabilities. Typical use cases currently don't exceed listening to music, managing alarms, and asking about weather (assuming non-smart home usage).
  2. While some users have expressed the need for an assistant to sound less robotic, there's still a whole lot of room for understanding what sounds too cheesy or artificial (e.g. the uncanny valley for voice).
  3. Figuring out the parameters of a correct answer before playing around with decoration (e.g. in terms of interesting additional editorial or personality) has been a useful approach.
