Streamlining Chatbot Developer
Workflow at Scale

Interaction Design, Information Design, CSS Styling, User Research

Introduction

In 2016, the Yahoo Search organization invested in launching chatbot experiments across Kik and Facebook Messenger (e.g. Yahoo News, MonkeyPet). The need for standardizing and streamlining bot creation amongst engineers, editors, and scientists arose as well as the ambition to release our in-house technology externally to third party developers. I took on the role of lead product designer where I partnered closely with groups of technology experts to deliver a minimum viable platform that enables an end-to-end workflow of creating rich dialog design and configuring natural language understanding. I helped streamline an initial toolset and drove visibility to the platform, which garnered 41 hack submissions across 5 Yahoo offices worldwide. We had shipped a small invite-only beta and later discontinued efforts after 1 year of intense experimentation.


The Workspace

At a glance we see the essentials of creating a chatbot in the navigation: means to design custom dialogs, to depict complex decision trees, to bundle mapped intents and utterances, to define entities, and to configure fulfillers. The workspace also supports ease of training and deploying a bot to a variety of chatbot platforms. In the main content area of this diagram, we see an example card of tokenizing a user utterance where a bot developer/trainer can refine the training of a model.



Technology Investigation

Early into the project, I examined the workflow the engineers had created via commandline to understand painpoints around learnability and usability behind creating a bot from scratch. I also surveyed competing platforms and technologies to learn the language and constructs.



Conceptual Model

I worked with lead engineers in creating a mental model around bots and skills. We wanted to design a system that encapsulated how various teams could leverage shared logic for concepts such as intents, fulfillment, and card design. We devised possibilities to package bot logic in terms of Skills and deploy bots across any chat platform in parallel.



Designing with Technology Experts

Here is an early discussion snapshot I had with some lead engineers in discussing conditional random field tagging interactions and some concepts on bulk review workflows. Card System

Most of the modules within the platform design are cards. In order to design cards that show consistency in aesthetic and interaction, I first created them in abstract form. I sectioned off where card content vs relevant actions needed to be defined with respect to context (e.g. use case, layout, relationship to other cards). Also, I factored in how cards needed to be stacked depending on the need for information density.

I have a couple of card types shown here: for Utterances, Intents/Skills, Dialogs, and the Bot Dashboard.

Lessons Learned

  1. I had to navigate many perspectives coming from various engineering teams, scientists, product managers, our technical writer, and editors. Most of my stakeholders lacked the visual vocabulary to articulate intricate technical details, so I often designed in lower fidelity with them to quickly and iteratively tease out the constraints.
  2. Though it’s presumed that this platform has layers of detail and dependencies, I focused on uncovering truly critical themes to understand target audience and the competitive strengths of our product. Much of my insight into framing a narrowed scope for the purposes of learning quickly stemmed from my Hive project.
